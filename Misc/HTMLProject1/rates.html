Keeling Curve

The Keeling Curve is a picture of the atmospheric CO2 levels. Unlike you might think, although the rise of CO2 happens on a fairly predictable basis, it varies slightly based on the season and actually goes down during the summer. Normally, it would look like a zigzag, going up from October to April and then dropping back down from May to September. This is because plants are absorbing the CO2 from the air. Although there is still CO2 being absorbed, it is not enough to slow it down, so it looks like a "one step down and two steps up" graph.

Image


As you can tell by looking at the image closely, CO2 levels rise by an average of 1.78 parts per million per year. Although you may not be able to tell easily, it is accelerating as you go up. According to a prominent environmental website, CO2 output accelerated, with the increase happening the most rapidly in the previous decade (2005-2014) with an average of 2.14 ppm per year. Though a year may seem like a fairly long time especially if you're young, even though a year is relatively small on both human life and global warming scales, this is happening 10 times faster than at any time that we have ever seen in the history of the planet. Years add up, and they have added up to more than 100 parts per million over just 60 years.
Although the majority of this will be in the what you can do section, it is important to talk about the CO2 level in the future. The level of 500 parts per million will probably be reached in my lifetime. But, when (or if) that number is reached depends on a variety of things. It depends on the public's willingness to change the tide.
According to this image, which we seem to be emulating as of now, we will reach that level around 2037. If our world upgrades our people to larger places and out of poverty within the next decade without doing enough about the global warming, it will come in the next decade. But, if we do try hard to fix it, we could push it back to 2060 or even further, allowing us more time to study other planets like Mars. Methane

Methane is a gas that has higher greenhouse gas potential than methane. There are three main sources of methane emission: cattle, fracking, and the Arctic. The methane is released in all three sources by the gas escaping from the locations where it was created or stored.

Human Population

The population of humanity is increasing at a rapid and possibly alarming rate. This increase is happening in both developed and developing countries. This population growth was mainly happening in the countries China, the US, and India, which also happen to be the biggest CO2 emitters. There is not just one reason that this is a problem, but it is related to global warming.

Why this is a problem

As a basic law of mathematics, as the number of something (it doesn't matter if it is the number of animals, humans, or computers) grows, assuming that they have the same level of efficiency, they will need to grow their consumption of resources. This leads to a higher global food intake which, combined with shrinking food growth area, may cause environmental stress.
When there are more people, there is less food for each person assuming the same amount of land is being farmed. This means that there is more stress on people's ability to eat. This may lead to rationing of food consumption. Yes, this probably will reduce obesity rates. But, it will also take food away from those who need it most: children, teenagers, and the disadvantaged in society.

Earth Overshoot Day

The Earth Overshoot Day is a program that is used to show how much we are "in debt" to the Earth. Forget the national debt, that debt can always be increased. Since roughly 1965, we have been using more resources than we need to. The "overshoot day" is a symbolic metric that shows how long it takes, starting on New Year's Day, to use as much as the earth makes in a year.

According to this picture, the Earth's human usage has been exceeded 1.7 times. That means, according to Earth Overshoot Day, we used 71% (171% of an Earth) more resources than we can make as a planet. This metric is particularly interesting because it shows not just how much we emit but when it exceeds the planet's capability and how much time it takes to do so.
